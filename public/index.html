The U.S. Presidential Election Deep Dive

Every four years, millions of Americans come together to cast their votes and choose the next President of the United States. However, the full process of selecting candidates, collecting votes, and determining a winner is a lot more intricate than a simple majority rule. We aim to educate our viewers, both beginners and experts alike, on the entire election process from beginning to end. By providing a clear overview of the underlying mechanisms that make our democracy work, we hope to empower our viewers to get more involved in the democratic process, and build a cohesive framework for understanding important real-world political events.

In this exploratory visualization, we are going to explore all key aspects of the US Presidential Election, from the Primaries, the Electoral College, the Election Day, and finally the Inauguration. To provide a backdrop for this journey, we'll be seeing how each of these core components factored into the 2020 election.

Background

The 2020 presidential election in the United States was a pivotal moment that resonated globally. Against a backdrop of intense political polarization, socio-economic upheaval, and the enduring effects of a worldwide pandemic, this election stood as a defining crossroads for the nation. The incumbent, President Donald Trump, sought re-election, championing a platform of economic growth, conservative policies, and a promise to restore America's greatness. Opposing him was former Vice President Joe Biden, advocating for unity, social justice, healthcare reform, and a comprehensive approach to combating the COVID-19 crisis.

The election was conducted amidst unprecedented challenges, including the COVID-19 pandemic, which altered the dynamics of traditional campaigning, prompting increased reliance on virtual communication and mail-in voting. Moreover, concerns about the integrity of the electoral process added to the tension, leading to intense scrutiny and debate over mail-in ballots, voter suppression, and the overall reliability of the election results.

The outcome of presidential elections not only shape domestic policies and the course of the nation but also reverberate globally, influencing diplomatic relations, international commitments, and the trajectory of global issues like climate change, trade, and human rights.

The Primaries

The presidential primaries are a crucial component of the United States' democratic process, serving as a means for political parties to select their respective candidates for the presidency. Primary elections occur in all 50 states, the District of Columbia, and U.S. territories.

The goal of each primary is to accumulate delegates for a particular candidate. Delegates are individuals chosen to represent their state/territory at the party's national convention, where a candidate will officially be nominated for the presidency. The number of delegates in each state are determined by population and political significance, and are assigned to candidates based on the percentage of votes received. The ultimate goal of the primary process is to secure a majority of delegates needed to become the party's nominee for president.

In 2020, both Democrats and Republicans hosted primaries, however we will focus only on the Democratic Primaries since the Republicans had an incumbent president running in Donald Trump. Trump won all 56 of the Republican Primaries, taking nearly 94% of the votes.

The Democratic Party Presidential Primaries and Caucuses took place from February 3rd to August 11th. A total of 3,979 pledged delegates were selected to the 2020 Democratic National Convention held from August 17th to 20th. A total of 29 major candidates declared their candidacies, the largest field of presidential primary candidates for any American political party in history.

The Electoral College

Once the Democrat and Republican candidates are chosen in the primaries, it's time for them to begin campaigning. On Election Day, the country comes together to choose the president for the next four years, but it's not quite as simple as a popular vote. To win the election, one candidate must win more electoral college votes than their opponent. But what does that mean, exactly?

The Electoral College is a system used in the United States to determine the outcome of presidential elections. Rather than electing the president directly through a popular vote, the Electoral College involves a process where each state is allocated a certain number of electors based on its representation in Congress. These electors, equal to the number of senators and representatives a state has, then cast their votes for the president.

Here's how it works:


  1. Allocation of Electors:
    Each state has a set number of electors, which is determined by the total number of senators (always two per state) plus the number of representatives in the House (based on the state's population). For example, Washington has 2 delegates in the Senate and 10 delegates in the House of Representatives, so Washington has 12 electors.
  2. Winner-Takes-All (in Most States):
    Most states follow a "winner-takes-all" system, where the candidate who wins the popular vote in that state gets all of its electoral votes. However, Maine and Nebraska allocate their electoral votes based on the winner of each congressional district. Also, sometimes electors don't vote for the party that wins the state's popular vote. Electors who do this are called "faithless electors," but they've never swung a presidential election in the past.
  3. 270 Electoral Votes:
    To win the presidency, a candidate needs to secure a majority of the electoral votes, which is currently 270 out of 538 total electors. If no candidate reaches this majority, the House of Representatives decides the outcome from the top three candidates.
  4. Meeting of Electors:
    After the general election, typically in December, the electors meet in their respective states to cast their votes for president and vice president. These votes are then transmitted to Congress to be officially counted and certified.
  5. Congressional Confirmation:
    In January, during a joint session of Congress, the electoral votes are counted and officially confirmed. The candidate who receives at least 270 electoral votes is declared the winner and becomes the next president.

This situation has benefits and detriments - while detractors claim that it goes against the democratic spirit, proponents claim that it allows for rural representation when urban dwellers are detached from important issues in sparsely populated areas. Regardless of individual views, the electoral college is currently an essential facet of the presidential election process, and one that is worth thoroughly understanding.

Swing states, also known as battleground states or purple states, play a crucial role in determining the outcome of elections, especially in the context of the Electoral College system in the United States.


These states are termed "swing" because they don't consistently vote for a particular political party. Their significance lies in their ability to potentially tip the balance of electoral votes in favor of one candidate or another.

Several factors contribute to a state being labeled a swing state:


  • Narrow Margins:
    Swing states often have a relatively even split between voters affiliated with different political parties. This balance makes their electoral votes highly contested, as even small shifts in voter preferences can sway the results.
  • Diverse Demographics:
    These states often represent a broad spectrum of demographics, including urban and rural areas, different ethnicities, income levels, and education backgrounds. As a result, they reflect a more diverse range of political opinions and interests.
  • Historical Voting Patterns:
    While swing states may have leaned toward a particular party in the past, their allegiance can change over time due to evolving demographics, shifting policy priorities, or changes in party platforms.

Swing states are crucial in election campaigns because winning them can significantly impact a candidate's path to securing the necessary 270 electoral votes to win the presidency. Consequently, these states receive heightened attention and campaign resources from candidates seeking to sway undecided voters or secure a marginal advantage. Candidates often tailor their policies, messaging, and campaign strategies to appeal specifically to voters in these swing states, recognizing the potential to win over crucial electoral votes that could make or break their chances of winning the presidency.

Election Day

The first Tuesday after the first Monday in November is Election Day: in 2020, that day was November 3rd.

On the day of the election, people from across the country submit their ballots and the tallies begin - but of course, it's a bit more complicated than that.

There are four main types of votes cast during the presidential election:

  • Absentee:
    This type of voting allows voters to cast their ballot by mail or in person before the election day. It's primarily designed for those who cannot be present at their polling place on the day of the election, for reasons such as being out of the country or having a disability. Voters usually need to request an absentee ballot before a specified deadline.
  • Early:
    Early voting allows voters to cast their ballots in person at a designated polling place before the official election day. The timeframe for early voting can vary from a few days to several weeks, depending on the jurisdiction. This method is designed to increase voter participation and reduce congestion at polling places on election day.
  • Election Day:
    This is the standard form of voting, where voters go to their assigned polling place on the day of the election. They cast their ballot in person, usually via electronic voting machines, punch cards, or paper ballots.
  • Provisional:
    Provisional ballots are used when there's some question about a voter's eligibility. This could happen if the voter's name is not on the electoral roll, if they don't have proper identification, or if they've moved and didn't update their voter registration. Provisional ballots are kept separate from regular ballots. After the election, election officials determine the voter's eligibility, and if eligible, the vote is counted.

Each of these voting methods is an integral part of the electoral process, offering flexibility and ensuring that as many eligible voters as possible have the opportunity to participate in elections.

Due to the multitude of voting options available to the American public, and due to the fact that there are over 100 million votes cast every single election, it can often take a while to get the full count. In many states, absentee and provisional ballots an be received several days after the election, as long as they are postmarked by election day - significantly extending the tallying process. Additionally, some states may also have recount laws that automatically trigger a recount if the margin of victory is within a certain percentage.

Once the vote-counting process in a given state is completed (a process which might take weeks), the state will be officially called in favor of a candidate. While the media might show projections on Election Day regarding winners for each state, this information is based off incomplete data, as many of the votes have yet to be counted. In close races, especially in swing states, media outlets might be especially cautious about prematurely calling a state for a candidate. In some states, where the margin of victory for a given candidate is high enough, the winner can be easily predicted, but even then, the legal process of calling a state will not occur until several weeks after the election.

Inauguration

Once all the individual votes are counted, the states certify the results, and the electors cast their votes, the Electoral College results are sent to Congress to be officially counted during a joint session in the first week of January.


The winner of the presidential election is officially declared during the joint congressional setting - whichever candidate gets at least 270 out of the 538 available electoral votes is the new President-elect. On Inauguration Day (January 20th/21st if the 20th falls on a Sunday), the new President of the United States is sworn into office.


The office of the President in the United States is perhaps the most powerful position any individual can attain in the modern world. To ensure that anyone who ascends to such high office is up to the task of representing the American people, a long series of events take place, giving every American citizen of voting age a chance to participate in one of the world's largest democratic processes. While the system may seem confusing at times, and while there are certainly flaws, a thorough understanding of the component elements can go a long way towards ensuring that our democracy continues to operate smoothly - a goal that everyone, on both sides of the aisle, can hope to strive for.
